# Skill - Executive Thought Leadership Rewriter with Review Loops

## Purpose
Rewrite a thought-leadership draft into sharper, more commercial, more memorable, executive-ready content for senior strategy, consulting, and C-suite audiences.

## Trigger
Use this skill when the user wants to sharpen, compress, reposition, or fully rewrite a thought-leadership article for an executive audience and can provide both:
- the original draft
- reviewer, partner, stakeholder, or editor feedback

This skill complements [[Workflow - Thought Leadership Article Lifecycle]] when the rewrite needs to be agent-led rather than human-led.
For a dedicated proof-point and source-validation pass after the rewrite, pair it with [[Skill - Evidence Finding and Narrative Integration with Validation Loops]].

## Inputs

### Required
- original article draft
- reviewer, partner, stakeholder, or editor feedback

### Optional
- style references or benchmark articles
- target word count or reduction target
- intended publisher, sponsor, client, or firm context
- excluded organizations, competitors, or benchmark/source categories
- approved proof points, source links, or internal facts that may be cited

## Start Gate
- Do not begin the rewrite until both required inputs are provided.
- If optional inputs are missing, proceed without them.
- Before rewriting, ask whether the piece is being written for a specific firm, client, or publication and whether any organizations or source/benchmark categories should be excluded.
- If the draft is missing key context that materially blocks the rewrite, ask one focused clarification question.
- Treat the named "operating lenses" below as perspectives for the same agent unless the user explicitly asks for delegated agents.

## Output Package
1. intake summary
2. rewrite brief
3. proposed structure
4. first rewritten draft
5. reviewer critique round 1
6. revised draft round 1
7. reviewer critique round 2
8. revised draft round 2
9. evidence additions
10. final polished draft
11. short summary of what changed and why

## Operating Lenses
- Intake: confirm inputs received and summarize them.
- Brief Interpreter: convert comments into a concrete editorial brief.
- Structural Editor: redesign the outline before rewriting prose.
- Executive Writer: rewrite for senior executive readership.
- Executive Reader / Partner Reviewer: critique the rewrite twice.
- Evidence Editor: add only a few proof points that materially strengthen the piece.
- Final Polisher: tighten rhythm, headings, transitions, and close.

## Steps

### Phase 1 - Intake and Brief Creation
1. Read the original article and all reviewer feedback in full.
2. Summarize the current thesis, audience, and strongest original ideas.
3. Convert feedback into a concrete rewrite brief with:
   - what must stay
   - what must change
   - target audience
   - target tone and positioning
   - structural issues
   - repetition issues
   - success criteria
4. Identify conflicts inside the feedback and state how they will be resolved.

### Phase 2 - Structural Redesign
1. Diagnose overlapping sections, repeated ideas, weak openings, weak endings, and missing executive relevance.
2. Propose a tighter structure before rewriting prose.
3. Explicitly decide which sections stay, merge, or get cut.
4. Decide whether the piece needs an "In brief" section, new title options, a hook-style subtitle, or a sharper closing frame.

### Phase 3 - First Rewrite
1. Rewrite against the new structure, not the old flow.
2. Preserve the core thesis and strongest original ideas.
3. Cut repetition aggressively.
4. Make each section do one job only.
5. Ensure the draft reaches a clear "what leaders should do now" message.

### Phase 4 - Review Loop 1
1. Run an Executive Reader / Partner Reviewer pass on the rewritten draft.
2. Critique executive tone, repetition, structure, readability, business relevance, section usefulness, opening strength, closing strength, and action agenda.
3. Name the top five changes still needed.
4. Revise the article based on that critique.

### Phase 5 - Review Loop 2
1. Run a second Executive Reader / Partner Reviewer pass on the revised draft.
2. Focus the second critique on what still feels generic, academic, slow, or insufficiently memorable.
3. Ensure this round is distinct from round 1 and not a restatement.
4. Revise again based on the second critique.

### Phase 6 - Evidence and Final Polish
1. Add two to five quantified proof points only where they materially strengthen the argument.
2. Use only user-provided, internally supplied, or otherwise validated evidence.
3. If no validated proof points are available, do not invent statistics. Instead, mark the best insert points and describe the evidence gap.
4. Polish for tone, section titles, flow, citation/source placement, and final paragraph strength.

## Tone Rules
- Write for CEOs, C-suite leaders, operators, and partners.
- Use strong verbs and decisive, commercial wording.
- Keep subject and verb near the start of sentences.
- Prefer commercial terms such as pricing power, churn, renewal, switching, business model exposure, value proposition, margin pressure, relevance, and profit pool when they fit the argument.
- Avoid academic, essayistic, or over-explanatory phrasing.
- The final piece should feel like practical executive thought leadership, not a polished school essay.

## Structural Rules
- Opening must make a sharp strategic claim quickly.
- Default opening architecture:
  - title
  - one-sentence subtitle that acts as a hook and gives the reader a reason to continue
  - two or three short opening sentences that sharpen the stakes
  - concise executive summary or "In brief" section
- Keep the text before "In brief" intentionally short.
- Include a concise executive summary or "In brief" section where useful.
- Each section must advance the argument.
- Avoid multiple sections that say the same thing in different words.
- Cut aggressively from the middle if the article drags.
- End with a practical leadership message, not a reflection.

## Quality Checks
- [ ] Both required inputs were provided before rewrite work started
- [ ] Feedback was translated into a concrete rewrite brief
- [ ] Structure was redesigned before prose was rewritten
- [ ] At least two distinct critique and revision loops were completed
- [ ] Core thesis and strongest original ideas were preserved
- [ ] Repetition was cut materially
- [ ] Final draft has a sharper opening and stronger ending
- [ ] Action agenda is concrete and executive-relevant
- [ ] No facts, examples, or proof points were invented
